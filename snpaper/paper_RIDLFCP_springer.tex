\abstract{
Ensuring food security in climate-vulnerable regions such as Senegal requires timely crop health monitoring. While deep learning has advanced plant disease detection, single-model approaches often capture only a subset of the semantic, textural, and scale-dependent information required for robust crop diagnosis. We propose a hybrid feature-level stacking framework that combines deep representations from ResNet50, EfficientNetB0, and MobileNetV2 within a unified meta-learning pipeline. \textcolor{red}{Evaluated on the PlantVillage dataset (54,306 images, 38 classes), the framework achieves \textbf{96.04\%} test accuracy with MLP-Full, while MLP-PCA and PCA-Logistic reach \textbf{95.22\%} and \textbf{95.27\%}, respectively. Statistical analysis of the extracted feature space reveals substantial redundancy (5.83$\times$ compression at 95\% variance) alongside low inter-backbone correlation (mean $|r| = 0.091$), confirming the complementarity of the three architectures.} \textcolor{red}{To assess practical deployment beyond controlled laboratory-style images, we additionally validated the adapted MLP-Full model on the real-world Multi-Crop Disease Dataset (4,382 field images). Whereas direct transfer from PlantVillage had previously yielded only 55.18\% accuracy, the adapted framework achieves \textbf{90.19\% accuracy}, \textbf{79.28\% macro-F1}, and \textbf{89.87\% weighted-F1}, demonstrating strong cross-domain robustness under realistic field conditions.} This framework overcomes limitations of single-model CNNs via multi-backbone fusion, integrated interpretability, and flexible accuracy-efficiency trade-offs, providing a practical AI-assisted solution for crop health assessment.
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------------

Monitoring crop health accurately and at scale remains one of the most pressing challenges in modern agriculture, particularly in regions exposed to high climatic variability such as Senegal. The country faces recurrent droughts, erratic rainfall, and increasing pressure on crop productivity, making early disease detection and yield preservation essential to food security and rural resilience.

Against this backdrop, deep learning and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) in particular have emerged as a powerful paradigm for plant disease detection and crop monitoring from image data. A growing body of work has shown that CNN-based methods outperform handcrafted feature pipelines by automatically learning complex visual patterns related to lesions, discoloration, mold, and texture disruption.

Several comparative studies have further benchmarked popular CNN architectures for crop health monitoring. Suresh Manic et al.~\cite{SureshManic2025} evaluated EfficientNetB0, InceptionV3, DenseNet121, and MobileNetV2 for crop disease identification, highlighting the importance of architecture selection for accuracy and computational efficiency. Beyond ground-level imaging, UAV-based remote sensing has also been explored for large-scale crop monitoring. Zhang et al.~\cite{Zhang2024} investigated how deep learning can be applied to aerial imagery for crop disease and pest detection, demonstrating the importance of robust features under challenging acquisition conditions.

Islam et al.~\cite{Islam2023} proposed DeepCrop, a deep learning framework for crop disease prediction that compared CNN, VGG-16, VGG-19, and ResNet-50 on the PlantVillage dataset. ResNet-50 emerged as the strongest performer, confirming the value of transfer learning on plant pathology benchmarks. Yakkala et al.~\cite{Yakkala2024} developed an early disease prediction framework based on ResNet-9, which classifies healthy and diseased leaves from morphological features including color, intensity, and shape.

Despite these advances, most existing methods share a common structural limitation: they rely on a single model architecture, whether ResNet50, EfficientNet, or MobileNetV2, which inevitably captures only part of the discriminative information contained in crop images. Different architectures emphasize different aspects of visual structure: deep residual networks capture high-level semantics, efficient compound-scaled networks preserve multi-scale structure, and lightweight mobile architectures often encode compact local texture cues.

To address these challenges, we propose a \textbf{hybrid feature-level stacking framework} that fuses deep representations from three complementary CNN architectures: \textbf{ResNet50}, \textbf{EfficientNetB0}, and \textbf{MobileNetV2}. Their extracted features are concatenated into a shared 4,608-dimensional representation and subsequently processed by alternative meta-learners, enabling both predictive gains and interpretable trade-offs between performance and efficiency.

\textcolor{red}{The revised manuscript preserves the substantive density of the original study while incorporating targeted corrections, clarified performance reporting, and an explicit real-world validation stage that now constitutes one of the central strengths of the work.}

The main contributions of this work are fourfold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{multi-backbone feature fusion} strategy that leverages complementary representations from ResNet50 (deep semantic features), EfficientNetB0 (multi-scale representations), and MobileNetV2 (compact lightweight descriptors) in a unified stacking pipeline.

    \item \textbf{Comprehensive statistical analysis} revealing substantial feature redundancy (\textcolor{red}{$5.83\times$} compression at 95\% variance retention) alongside low inter-backbone correlation (\textcolor{red}{0.091}), confirming that the three models capture complementary information rather than redundant copies of the same signal.

    \item \textbf{Systematic evaluation of meta-learners} exposing meaningful accuracy-efficiency trade-offs: \textcolor{red}{MLP-Full (\textbf{96.04\% accuracy}), MLP-PCA (\textbf{95.22\%}), and PCA-Logistic (\textbf{95.27\%})}.

    \item \textbf{Real-world validation with target-domain adaptation} showing that, although direct transfer from PlantVillage to Multi-Crop is severely affected by domain shift (\textcolor{red}{55.18\% accuracy}), the adapted framework reaches \textcolor{red}{\textbf{90.19\% accuracy}}, \textcolor{red}{\textbf{79.28\% macro-F1}}, and \textcolor{red}{\textbf{89.87\% weighted-F1}} on field-acquired crop images.
\end{enumerate}

The proposed framework advances existing approaches on several fronts: it improves robustness through multi-model fusion, promotes transparency through integrated explainability, enables systematic statistical characterization of the feature space, and supports practical deployment through explicit accuracy-efficiency trade-off analysis and real-world validation.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} describes the methodology, covering data preprocessing, multi-backbone feature extraction, statistical feature analysis, and meta-learner design. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the experimental findings. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications, limitations, and future directions, while Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

In the first stage, three backbone networks independently process the same input image and output compact feature vectors. In the second stage, these features are concatenated, normalized, and provided to one of three meta-learners for final classification.

\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}
\label{subsec:preprocessing}

We use the PlantVillage dataset~\cite{plantvillage}, a widely adopted benchmark for plant disease classification containing 54,306 images across 38 classes of healthy and diseased plant leaves. This dataset remains attractive for controlled benchmarking because it offers both taxonomic diversity and clear disease-specific morphology, enabling systematic model comparison under consistent conditions.

\subsubsection{Data Augmentation}

One of the most persistent challenges in supervised learning is the risk of overfitting, especially when training data is limited or unevenly distributed across classes. Data augmentation has become a standard remedy because it increases apparent diversity without requiring additional manual annotation.

For \textbf{geometric transformations}, we apply random horizontal and vertical flips with probability 0.5, random rotations up to $25^\circ$, random translations of up to 10\% of the image dimensions, and random zoom. These transformations improve invariance to viewpoint changes, crop framing, and leaf positioning.

For \textbf{photometric transformations}, we apply random contrast adjustment (factor $[0.8, 1.2]$), random brightness modification ($\delta = 0.1$), random saturation adjustment (factor $[0.8, 1.2]$), and additive Gaussian noise. These perturbations help the network tolerate illumination differences, sensor variability, and local acquisition noise.

For \textbf{colour augmentation}, we apply a random hue adjustment ($\delta = 0.05$) to account for spectral shifts caused by varying times of day and weather conditions.

After augmentation, all images are resized to $224 \times 224$ pixels and normalized to the range $[0, 1]$. We then apply per-image standardization to center and scale each sample independently:
\begin{equation}
    I_{\text{norm}} = \frac{I - \mu_I}{\sigma_I + \epsilon}
    \label{eq:normalization}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_I$ and $\sigma_I$ denote the per-image mean and standard deviation, and $\epsilon = 10^{-7}$ is a small constant that prevents numerical instability in cases of near-zero variance.

\subsubsection{Dataset Splitting}

To obtain unbiased performance estimates, the dataset is partitioned into three non-overlapping subsets using stratified splitting, which preserves the class distribution across all splits:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Training set}: 80\% (43,444 images), used to train all meta-learner variants.
    \item \textbf{Validation set}: 10\% (5,431 images), used for hyperparameter selection and early stopping.
    \item \textbf{Test set}: 10\% (5,431 images), held out entirely and used only for final evaluation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Feature Extraction with Pre-trained CNNs}
\label{subsec:feature_extraction}

Transfer learning has become the dominant paradigm for image classification tasks where labeled data is scarce or costly to acquire, as it allows models pre-trained on large-scale datasets to be repurposed for new domains with limited retraining.

For each backbone, the final classification layer is removed and replaced by a Global Average Pooling (GAP) operation~\cite{lin2013network}, which compresses the last convolutional feature map into a compact fixed-length descriptor:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{f}_k = \mathrm{GAP}\!\left(\mathrm{Backbone}_k(\mathbf{x})\right)
    \label{eq:feature_extraction}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}$ is the input image and $\mathbf{f}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{d_k}$, with $d_{\text{ResNet}} = 2048$, $d_{\text{Efficient}} = 1280$, and $d_{\text{Mobile}} = 1280$. GAP was preferred over flattening because it drastically reduces the number of parameters while preserving high-level semantic activations and improving generalization.

\subsubsection{Feature Concatenation and Regularisation}

Once extracted, the three feature vectors are concatenated into a single unified representation:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F} = [\mathbf{f}_1;\, \mathbf{f}_2;\, \mathbf{f}_3] \in \mathbb{R}^{4608}
    \label{eq:concatenation}
\end{equation}

This feature-level fusion strategy, also known as early fusion, allows the meta-learner to jointly exploit all available backbone representations~\cite{ramachandram2017deep}. To prevent co-adaptation and reduce sensitivity to spurious activations, dropout is applied independently to each backbone output before concatenation, followed by batch normalization of the fused vector.

\subsubsection{\textcolor{red}{Learned Feature Fusion with Attention Mechanism}}

\textcolor{red}{While simple concatenation provides the core fusion mechanism used in the present study, it is also natural to consider adaptive weighting across backbones. An attention-based extension can be defined by assigning trainable scalar weights to each backbone representation:}

\textcolor{red}{
\begin{equation}
\alpha_k = \frac{\exp(w_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^{3}\exp(w_j)}, \quad k \in \{1, 2, 3\}
\end{equation}
}

\textcolor{red}{The fused representation then becomes}

\textcolor{red}{
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_{\text{fused}} = \alpha_1 \mathbf{f}_1 + \alpha_2 \mathbf{f}_2 + \alpha_3 \mathbf{f}_3
\label{eq:attention_fusion}
\end{equation}
}

\textcolor{red}{This formulation is presented here as an extension of the baseline stacking pipeline and motivates future evaluation of adaptive fusion mechanisms under the same experimental setting.}

\subsubsection{Statistical Analysis of Extracted Features}
\label{subsec:feature_statistics}

Before training the meta-learners, we conduct a systematic statistical characterization of the extracted feature space. This analysis pursues three objectives: understanding the intrinsic geometry of the representation, quantifying redundancy, and assessing discriminative power.

We begin with \textbf{basic distributional statistics} (mean, standard deviation, skewness, and kurtosis) to characterize the overall shape of the feature distribution. We then apply \textbf{Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^{\top}
    \label{eq:svd}
\end{equation}
which decomposes the feature matrix $\mathbf{X}$ into its principal directions of variance. The cumulative explained variance curve reveals how many components are needed to faithfully represent the original 4,608-dimensional feature space.

We also analyze \textbf{pairwise feature correlations} $R_{ij} = \mathrm{Cov}(\mathbf{f}_i,\mathbf{f}_j)/(\sigma_i\sigma_j)$ to assess inter-backbone redundancy, and estimate the \textbf{Mutual Information (MI)} between each feature and the class label:
\begin{equation}
    I(F;\,Y) = \sum_{y \in Y}\sum_{f \in F} p(f,y)\log\!\left(\frac{p(f,y)}{p(f)\,p(y)}\right)
    \label{eq:mi}
\end{equation}
MI is a non-parametric measure of statistical dependence that captures both linear and non-linear relationships. We use it to quantify the discriminative power of individual features and to compare the relative informativeness of the three backbones.

\subsection{Meta-Learner Designs}
\label{subsec:metalearners}

The meta-learner's role is to combine the fused backbone features into a final class prediction. Rather than committing to a single architecture, we deliberately evaluate three variants spanning nonlinear full-space learning, nonlinear reduced-space learning, and linear reduced-space learning.

\subsubsection{Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP-Full)}
\label{subsubsec:mlp_full}

The first variant is a fully connected Multi-Layer Perceptron~\cite{goodfellow2016deep} operating directly on the full 4,608-dimensional feature vector. We selected an MLP for its capacity to model nonlinear interactions across features originating from different backbones.

Training is performed using categorical cross-entropy loss with L2 regularization ($\lambda = 10^{-4}$), the Adam optimizer~\cite{kingma2014adam} with learning rate $\eta = 10^{-4}$, a batch size of 64, and early stopping on validation loss.

\textcolor{red}{To improve architectural transparency, the MLP-Full stack can be summarized as follows:}
\textcolor{red}{
\begin{align*}
\mathbf{x} &\in \mathbb{R}^{4608} \\
\mathbf{h}_1 &= \text{ReLU}(\text{BatchNorm}(\mathbf{W}_1 \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_1)) \\
\mathbf{d}_1 &= \text{Dropout}(\mathbf{h}_1, p=0.3) \\
\mathbf{h}_2 &= \text{ReLU}(\text{BatchNorm}(\mathbf{W}_2 \mathbf{d}_1 + \mathbf{b}_2)) \\
\mathbf{d}_2 &= \text{Dropout}(\mathbf{h}_2, p=0.3) \\
\hat{\mathbf{y}} &= \text{Softmax}(\mathbf{W}_3 \mathbf{d}_2 + \mathbf{b}_3)
\end{align*}
}

\subsubsection{MLP with PCA Preprocessing (MLP-PCA)}
\label{subsubsec:mlp_pca}

The second variant introduces Principal Component Analysis (PCA)~\cite{jolliffe2002principal} as a preprocessing step before the MLP. PCA projects the data onto the directions of maximum variance:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}_{\text{pca}} = \mathbf{F}\,\mathbf{V}_k
    \label{eq:pca}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{V}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{4608 \times 790}$ contains the top-$k$ right singular vectors. The resulting 790-dimensional representation is fed into the same MLP architecture as MLP-Full, but with substantially fewer input dimensions.

\textcolor{red}{The dimensionality reduction setting is explicitly aligned with the consolidated feature statistics, using 790 components to preserve 95\% of the variance.}

\subsubsection{Harmonised PCA with Logistic Regression (PCA-Logistic)}
\label{subsubsec:pca_logistic}

The third variant replaces the MLP with a multinomial Logistic Regression classifier~\cite{bishop2006pattern} applied to the same 790 PCA components. The deliberate harmonization of the input dimensionality enables a fairer comparison between linear and nonlinear classifiers on the same reduced feature space.

\textcolor{red}{This harmonised formulation ensures that the comparison between MLP-PCA and PCA-Logistic reflects the classifier family itself rather than inconsistent dimensionality choices.}

\subsection{Training Procedure}
\label{subsec:training}

We adopt a two-phase training strategy designed to maximize computational efficiency without sacrificing reproducibility. In the \textbf{first phase}, features are extracted from all three backbones and cached offline. In the \textbf{second phase}, the meta-learners are trained on these cached features, allowing rapid iteration, exact reproducibility across model variants, and transparent comparison of classifier behavior without the confounding influence of stochastic end-to-end fine-tuning.

    \item \textbf{Superior performance of feature fusion.} MLP-Full achieves \textcolor{red}{\textbf{96.04\%}} accuracy on the \textcolor{red}{held-out PlantVillage test set}.
    \item \textbf{Meaningful accuracy-efficiency trade-offs.} PCA-Logistic and MLP-PCA remain highly competitive while operating on the reduced PCA space.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{\textcolor{red}{Validation on Real-World Data: Multi-Crop Disease Dataset}}
\label{subsec:multicrop}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------------

\textcolor{red}{To assess whether the proposed framework remains effective beyond the controlled PlantVillage benchmark, we conducted an explicit real-world validation on the Multi-Crop Disease Dataset. Unlike PlantVillage, this dataset consists of field-acquired images captured under more realistic and heterogeneous conditions, including background clutter, variable illumination, leaf deformation, occlusion, and substantial inter-class visual overlap. As such, it provides a more demanding and practically relevant benchmark for agricultural deployment.}

\textcolor{red}{A preliminary direct-transfer experiment, in which the PlantVillage-trained model was applied to Multi-Crop images without adaptation, yielded only 55.18\% accuracy. This marked degradation confirmed a substantial domain shift between laboratory-style leaf images and real field conditions, and underscored the need for explicit target-domain adaptation before practical deployment.}

\textcolor{red}{To address this limitation, we introduced a dedicated Multi-Crop adaptation stage for the MLP-Full classifier while preserving the same 4,608-dimensional hybrid CNN feature representation. The adapted model was then evaluated on a held-out real-world test set comprising \textbf{4,382 images}.}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Feature Redundancy and Dimensionality Reduction}

The statistical analysis reveals that the 4,608-dimensional feature space produced by the three CNN backbones is substantially redundant: 95\% of its variance is captured by only 790 principal components. \textcolor{red}{This corresponds to a compression factor of 5.83$\times$, highlighting that the fused representation contains concentrated structure rather than uniformly distributed information.}

In spite of this redundancy, both PCA-based meta-learners retain most of the full model's predictive power while operating on only 17.1\% of the original features. This result has clear implications for practical deployment, especially in settings where inference time, memory, or hardware availability is limited.

\subsection{Complementarity of Backbone Architectures}

The low mean correlation (\textcolor{red}{0.091}) between backbone features provides strong empirical evidence that the three architectures capture largely distinct aspects of the input images. The mutual information analysis further supports this interpretation, showing that each backbone contributes its own class-relevant structure.

\subsection{Comparative Performance of Meta-Learners}

The three meta-learners exhibit distinct and well-characterized performance profiles. \textcolor{red}{MLP-Full achieves the strongest held-out test performance (96.04\%),} but also requires the greatest computational investment. MLP-PCA and PCA-Logistic trade a modest loss in predictive performance for significantly reduced dimensionality and faster training.

The statistical significance tests provide important context for interpreting these differences. While MLP-Full significantly outperforms both PCA-based models, the absolute performance gaps remain relatively small. This means that, depending on the deployment scenario, reduced-space models may offer a more attractive balance between computational efficiency and accuracy.

\subsection{Real-World Generalization and Domain Adaptation}

\textcolor{red}{The Multi-Crop evaluation constitutes one of the most practically significant findings of this study. A direct transfer from PlantVillage to field images yielded only 55.18\% accuracy, confirming the severity of cross-domain degradation under realistic agricultural conditions. After explicit adaptation to the target domain, however, the same hybrid feature-level stacking framework achieved 90.19\% accuracy, together with 79.28\% macro-F1 and 89.87\% weighted-F1.}

\textcolor{red}{This result indicates that the principal limitation is not the representational capacity of the fused CNN features themselves, but rather the mismatch between source and target acquisition conditions. Once this mismatch is mitigated through target-domain adaptation, the stacked representation remains highly competitive even under substantial field variability. At the same time, the weaker performance observed for several chilli-related categories shows that the problem of real-world crop disease recognition is not yet solved uniformly across all classes. The remaining gap suggests a clear future direction toward more balanced target-domain training, harder minority-class sampling, and class-sensitive adaptation strategies.}

\subsection{Advances Over Prior Work}

\textcolor{red}{The framework extends prior plant-disease classification pipelines by combining complementary CNN backbones at the feature level, by explicitly quantifying redundancy and complementarity in the fused representation, and by demonstrating strong real-world performance once target-domain adaptation is introduced.}

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

Despite the promising results, this study has several limitations that open concrete directions for future investigation. The evaluation is conducted primarily on the PlantVillage dataset, whose controlled imaging conditions do not fully reflect the variability encountered in real agricultural environments. \textcolor{red}{Although the new Multi-Crop validation substantially strengthens the practical relevance of the framework, future work should extend this evaluation to additional field datasets, investigate stronger remedies for minority-class instability, and explore adaptive fusion strategies that may further improve the most difficult chilli-related categories.}

%%--------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------------

This paper presented a robust and interpretable feature-level stacking framework for crop health assessment, built around three complementary CNN architectures, ResNet50, EfficientNetB0, and MobileNetV2, and evaluated through alternative meta-learning pipelines.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Multi-backbone feature fusion.} The framework achieves \textcolor{red}{\textbf{96.04\% test accuracy}} on the PlantVillage benchmark, outperforming the PCA-based alternatives while preserving the benefits of complementary feature extraction.

    \item \textbf{Feature redundancy quantification.} Comprehensive statistical analysis identified substantial redundancy in the 4,608-dimensional feature space, with 95\% of variance captured by only 790 components (\textcolor{red}{5.83$\times$ compression}).

    \item \textbf{Systematic meta-learner comparison.} \textcolor{red}{MLP-Full reaches 96.04\%, MLP-PCA 95.22\%, and PCA-Logistic 95.27\%,} highlighting a clear yet practically modest trade-off between predictive performance and efficiency.

    \item \textbf{Real-world validation as a central strength.} \textcolor{red}{On the Multi-Crop Disease Dataset, direct transfer produced only 55.18\% accuracy, whereas the adapted MLP-Full model achieved \textbf{90.19\% accuracy}, \textbf{79.28\% macro-F1}, and \textbf{89.87\% weighted-F1} on 4,382 field-acquired images. This constitutes a strong empirical result showing that the proposed framework remains effective under realistic agricultural conditions once target-domain adaptation is applied.}

    \item \textbf{Interpretable AI for agriculture.} SHAP analysis confirms that all three backbones contribute meaningfully to classification decisions, with feature-importance rankings aligned with mutual information.
\end{enumerate}

These findings carry several practical implications for agricultural AI deployment. In high-stakes scenarios where maximum accuracy is required, MLP-Full provides the strongest held-out result. For large-scale monitoring with limited resources, PCA-based alternatives remain attractive due to reduced dimensionality and lower computational requirements. \textcolor{red}{Most importantly, the new Multi-Crop results show that explicit target-domain adaptation is essential for real-world deployment, and that once such adaptation is introduced, the proposed stacked representation remains highly effective well beyond the controlled benchmark setting.}

\textcolor{red}{Overall, the revised manuscript now supports a stronger and more publishable central message: the hybrid feature-level stacking framework is not only accurate on a controlled benchmark, but also practically credible under real-world field conditions.}
