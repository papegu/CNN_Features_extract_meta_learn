\abstract{
\textcolor{red}{
Accurate crop health assessment is critical for food security in climate-vulnerable regions like Senegal. While deep learning shows promise for plant disease detection, existing approaches predominantly rely on single-model architectures that lack generalizability and interpretability, limiting real-world adoption. This study proposes a hybrid feature-level stacking framework combining three complementary pre-trained Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)—ResNet50, EfficientNetB0, and MobileNetV2—with learned attention-based fusion to improve classification performance. The framework extracts and fuses deep features processed by three meta-learner variants: a full Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), an MLP with Principal Component Analysis (PCA) preprocessing, and a harmonized PCA-Logistic pipeline using exactly 790 components. We integrate comprehensive statistical analysis, cross-validation, SHAP-based explainability, and robust cross-domain evaluation.

Evaluated on the PlantVillage dataset (54,306 images, 38 classes), our approach achieves \textbf{96.01\% mean test accuracy (±0.09\% via stratified 5-fold CV)} with the full MLP, outperforming individual backbones by 1.9--2.7\%. The learned attention fusion mechanism improves performance from 96.04\% (baseline concatenation) to \textbf{96.72\% (multi-head attention)}, demonstrating systematic gains from adaptive feature combination. Cross-domain evaluation on Multi-Crop (field images) reveals that direct transfer achieves only 55.18\%, but progressive unfreezing adaptation recovers performance to \textbf{75.34\% (+20.16\% improvement)}, demonstrating viability for real-world deployment. We report a comprehensive set of 10+ performance metrics (accuracy, precision, recall, F1, ROC-AUC, balanced accuracy, Hamming loss), meeting peer-review standards and enabling transparent model evaluation.

Our framework addresses key limitations through learned multi-model fusion, comprehensive 10+ metrics reporting, cross-domain robustness via domain adaptation, integrated explainability, stratified k-fold validation, and flexible accuracy--efficiency trade-offs, supporting reliable AI for agricultural monitoring in resource-constrained environments.
}
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------------

Accurate assessment of crop health and yield potential remains a major challenge in modern agriculture, particularly in regions with high climatic variability such as Senegal. The country experiences irregular rainfall distribution, recurrent droughts, and increasing temperature fluctuations, all of which significantly impact agricultural productivity and food security~\cite{koseki_dakar_2024}. Traditional approaches based on field observation and empirical estimation are often subjective, lack quantitative rigour, and fail to scale---making them inadequate for capturing complex interactions between climatic, environmental, and agronomic factors under increasing climate variability~\cite{misbah_multi-sensors_2021}.

In recent years, deep learning---particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)---has emerged as a powerful tool for plant disease detection and crop monitoring using image data. For instance, M.~Hussain et al.~\cite{mir_deep_2025} employed deep learning to strengthen IoT-based crop monitoring by automating greenhouse surveillance and environmental regulation. Their framework integrates sensor data with advanced activation functions (ELU, Swish, ReLU, SELU, and Mish), aiming to reduce human intervention and enhance sustainability. While promising, this approach remains confined to greenhouse environments and lacks scalability to open-field conditions.

Building on this momentum, several comparative studies have benchmarked popular CNN architectures for crop health monitoring. Suresh~Manic et al.~\cite{SureshManic2025} conducted a comparative analysis of plant disease detection using EfficientNetB0, InceptionV3, DenseNet121, and MobileNetV2. Their study demonstrated that EfficientNetB0 achieved the highest validation accuracy (96.4\%) with strong precision and recall, highlighting its suitability for real-time deployment on edge devices in resource-constrained agricultural environments. González-Briones et al.~\cite{GonzalezBriones2025} further confirmed that ResNet-50 and EfficientNet outperform standard CNNs when combined with explainability mechanisms, achieving 63.79\% and 98.27\% accuracy respectively on the PlantVillage dataset. These findings underline that the choice of CNN architecture involves trade-offs between accuracy, robustness, and computational efficiency, and that interpretability is increasingly recognised as a deployment requirement.

Beyond ground-based datasets, UAV-based remote sensing has also been explored. Zhang et al.~\cite{Zhang2024} investigated deep learning for detecting crop diseases and pests from UAV imagery. Their study highlights the potential of combining high-resolution aerial data with CNNs to achieve accurate, large-scale monitoring of plant health. However, challenges persist regarding data variability, model generalisation, and the computational demands of real-time UAV image processing.

Islam et al.~\cite{Islam2023} proposed DeepCrop, a deep learning-based framework for crop disease prediction. Their study compared CNN, VGG-16, VGG-19, and ResNet-50 architectures on the PlantVillage dataset, demonstrating that ResNet-50 achieved the highest accuracy (98.98\%) with robust cross-validation. Beyond model performance, they developed a web application enabling farmers to upload leaf images for real-time diagnosis and preliminary treatment suggestions. This work highlights the practical integration of advanced deep learning into accessible tools for smallholder farmers, bridging academic research and field deployment. However, its evaluation remains limited to controlled datasets.

In addition, Yakkala et al.~\cite{Yakkala2024} developed a deep learning-based framework for crop health enhancement through early disease prediction. Their study employed ResNet-9 to classify healthy and diseased leaves across multiple crops, leveraging morphological features such as colour, intensity, and dimensions. The model achieved high accuracy, supported by systematic preprocessing, augmentation, and cross-validation strategies. Beyond technical performance, the authors emphasised integration with environmentally friendly biological control methods, positioning their approach as both technologically advanced and ecologically sustainable.

Despite these advances, most existing methods rely on \textbf{single-model architectures}---such as ResNet50, EfficientNet, or MobileNetV2---which often fail to generalise effectively across different environments. Models trained on controlled datasets like PlantVillage frequently suffer from \textbf{domain shift} when applied to real-world agricultural conditions, including drone or satellite imagery. This limitation leads to overfitting and reduced robustness in practical deployment scenarios. Moreover, many studies focus on end-to-end classification without exploiting the \textbf{complementary strengths} of multiple models or providing sufficient \textbf{interpretability} of predictions. As a result, these approaches lack both robustness and transparency---essential qualities for real-world agricultural decision-making.

To address these challenges, this study proposes a \textbf{hybrid feature-level stacking framework} that combines multiple pre-trained CNN models to improve generalisation and predictive performance. Instead of relying on final predictions, our method extracts and fuses deep features from three complementary architectures---\textbf{ResNet50}, \textbf{EfficientNetB0}, and \textbf{MobileNetV2}---which are then processed by a meta-learner for final classification. We investigate three meta-learner variants: a full MLP, an MLP with PCA preprocessing, and a harmonized PCA-Logistic pipeline using exactly 790 components. The framework integrates comprehensive statistical analysis, regularisation, cross-validation, and paired statistical tests. To enhance transparency, we employ SHAP for global feature importance.

\textcolor{red}{
Crucially, our approach \textbf{departs fundamentally from conventional static stacking methodologies}. While existing ensemble CNN approaches typically employ naive concatenation or simple averaging of backbone features~\cite{ramachandram2017deep}, our framework introduces a \textbf{learnable attention-based fusion mechanism} that dynamically weights each backbone's contribution during training. Rather than treating all architectures equally, our method discovers the adaptive importance of ResNet50 (semantic information), EfficientNetB0 (multi-scale representations), and MobileNetV2 (texture patterns) through learned softmax-normalized attention coefficients. This adaptive weighting enables the meta-learner to discover optimal feature combination strategies tailored to the plant disease classification task.

Furthermore, our framework addresses domain shift robustness through systematic progressive unfreezing adaptation, enabling effective transfer from controlled laboratory datasets (PlantVillage) to challenging real-world agricultural conditions (Multi-Crop field imagery). Most transfer learning approaches fail catastrophically under domain shift (55.18\% accuracy on Multi-Crop direct transfer); our method recovers 75.34\% through staged fine-tuning with test-time augmentation. Beyond methodological innovation, our contribution integrates \textbf{four complementary technical advances}: (1) adaptive attention-based fusion replacing static concatenation, (2) cross-domain robustness validation with quantified domain adaptation results, (3) comprehensive transparent evaluation across 10+ performance dimensions, and (4) statistical rigor through stratified k-fold cross-validation with reproducibility metrics (96.01\% ± 0.09\%). This holistic integration of adaptive fusion, explainability, robustness, and statistical validation positions our work beyond conventional feature-level stacking.
}

The main contributions of this work are fourfold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{multi-backbone feature fusion} approach that leverages complementary representations from ResNet50 (deep semantic features), EfficientNetB0 (efficient multi-scale representations), and MobileNetV2 (lightweight texture patterns), achieving \textbf{95.82\% test accuracy}---outperforming any single backbone by 1.9--2.7\%.

    \item \textbf{Comprehensive statistical analysis} revealing substantial feature redundancy ($5.8\times$ compression at 95\% variance) and low inter-backbone correlation (0.086), confirming architectural complementarity with mean Mutual Information scores of 0.152 (ResNet50), 0.138 (EfficientNetB0), and 0.131 (MobileNetV2).

    \item \textbf{Systematic evaluation of meta-learners} demonstrating important trade-offs: MLP-Full (\textbf{95.82\% accuracy}), harmonized PCA-Logistic (\textbf{94.61\% accuracy}, $6.3\times$ faster training), and MLP-PCA (\textbf{94.86\% accuracy} with only 17.1\% of original features). McNemar's tests confirm that MLP-Full significantly outperforms PCA-based models ($p < 10^{-6}$), with no significant difference between MLP-PCA and PCA-Logistic ($p = 0.253$).

    \item \textbf{Integrated explainability} confirming that all three backbones contribute meaningfully to classification decisions, with models focusing on biologically relevant regions---lesions, spots, and discolouration patterns---rather than spurious background correlations.
\end{enumerate}

The proposed framework addresses key limitations of existing approaches by enhancing robustness through multi-model feature fusion, providing transparency through integrated explainability, enabling significant dimensionality reduction without performance loss, and offering flexible deployment options suited to different resource constraints. These advances contribute to reliable, interpretable AI systems for agricultural monitoring, supporting food security initiatives in resource-constrained environments like Senegal, where climate variability increasingly threatens crop productivity.

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} details our methodology, including data preprocessing, multi-backbone feature extraction, statistical feature analysis, meta-learner architectures, and validation strategies. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents experimental results, including feature statistics, model performance comparisons, statistical significance tests, and interpretability analysis. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the implications of our findings, limitations, and future work. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with key contributions and practical recommendations for deployment.

\subsection{Overall Architecture}
\label{subsec:architecture}

Our framework follows a two-stage hierarchical architecture designed to leverage complementary representations from multiple pre-trained CNNs. In the first stage, three distinct CNN architectures serve as feature extractors, transforming input images into high-dimensional feature vectors. In the second stage, we investigate three meta-learner variants: a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) operating on all features, an MLP with PCA preprocessing, and a harmonized PCA-Logistic Regression pipeline using exactly 790 components. This multi-learner comparison allows us to systematically evaluate the trade-offs between representational capacity, computational efficiency, and statistical robustness.

\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}
\label{subsec:preprocessing}

We use the PlantVillage dataset~\cite{plantvillage}, a benchmark collection for agricultural disease classification containing 54,306 images across 38 classes of healthy and diseased plant leaves. The dataset covers 14 crop species---apple, blueberry, cherry, corn, grape, orange, peach, pepper, potato, raspberry, soybean, squash, strawberry, and tomato---with multiple disease conditions per crop.

\subsubsection{Data Augmentation}

A well-known challenge in supervised learning is the risk of overfitting, particularly when training data is limited or imbalanced. Data augmentation has been widely adopted as an effective remedy: by artificially expanding the training distribution, it forces models to learn invariant representations rather than memorising specific image instances~\cite{shorten2019survey}. Following this principle, we design a multi-branch augmentation pipeline that operates on three complementary axes.

For \textbf{geometric transformations}, we apply random horizontal and vertical flips (probability 0.5), random rotations up to $25^\circ$, random translations up to 10\% of the image dimensions, and random zoom within the range $[0.8, 1.2]$. These operations simulate the variability in plant orientation and capture distance that naturally occurs in field photography.

For \textbf{photometric transformations}, we apply random contrast adjustment (factor $[0.8, 1.2]$), random brightness modification ($\delta = 0.1$), random saturation adjustment (factor $[0.8, 1.2]$), and Gaussian noise injection ($\sigma = 0.01$). These perturbations replicate the effect of changing lighting conditions and sensor noise, two sources of variability that are particularly prevalent in outdoor agricultural imaging~\cite{mohanty2016using}.

For \textbf{colour augmentation}, we apply a random hue adjustment ($\delta = 0.05$) to account for the spectral shifts caused by different times of day and weather conditions.

After augmentation, all images are resized to $224 \times 224$ pixels and normalised to the range $[0, 1]$. We then apply per-image standardisation to centre and scale each sample independently:
\begin{equation}
    I_{\text{norm}} = \frac{I - \mu_I}{\sigma_I + \epsilon}
    \label{eq:normalization}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_I$ and $\sigma_I$ denote the per-image mean and standard deviation respectively, and $\epsilon = 10^{-7}$ is a small constant that prevents numerical instability in the case of near-zero variance.

\subsubsection{Dataset Splitting}

To obtain unbiased performance estimates, we partition the dataset into three non-overlapping subsets using stratified splitting, which preserves the class distribution across all splits~\cite{kohavi1995study}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Training set}: 80\% (43,444 images) — used to train all meta-learner variants.
    \item \textbf{Validation set}: 10\% (5,431 images) — used for hyperparameter selection and early stopping.
    \item \textbf{Test set}: 10\% (5,431 images) — held out entirely and used only for final evaluation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Feature Extraction with Pre-trained CNNs}
\label{subsec:feature_extraction}

Transfer learning has become the dominant paradigm for image classification tasks where labelled data is scarce, as it allows models pre-trained on large-scale datasets to be repurposed for new domains with minimal additional training~\cite{pan2010survey}. Building on this principle, we use three CNN architectures pre-trained on ImageNet~\cite{deng2009imagenet} as fixed feature extractors: ResNet50~\cite{resnet}, EfficientNetB0~\cite{efficientnet}, and MobileNetV2~\cite{mobilenetv2}. Each was selected for its distinct architectural philosophy, as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:intro}, and together they provide a complementary set of visual representations.

For each backbone, we remove the final classification layer and attach a Global Average Pooling (GAP) operation~\cite{lin2013network}, which compresses the last convolutional feature map into a compact, fixed-length descriptor:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{f}_k = \mathrm{GAP}\!\left(\mathrm{Backbone}_k(\mathbf{x})\right)
    \label{eq:feature_extraction}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}$ is the input image and $\mathbf{f}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{d_k}$, with $d_{\text{ResNet}} = 2048$, $d_{\text{Efficient}} = 1280$, and $d_{\text{Mobile}} = 1280$. GAP was preferred over flattening because it significantly reduces the number of parameters and has been shown to act as a structural regulariser that improves generalisation~\cite{lin2013network}.

\subsubsection{Feature Concatenation and Regularisation}

Once extracted, the three feature vectors are concatenated into a single unified representation:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F} = [\mathbf{f}_1;\, \mathbf{f}_2;\, \mathbf{f}_3] \in \mathbb{R}^{4608}
    \label{eq:concatenation}
\end{equation}

This feature-level fusion strategy, also known as early fusion, allows the meta-learner to jointly exploit all available backbone representations~\cite{ramachandram2017deep}. To prevent co-adaptation between backbone features and reduce overfitting, we apply dropout regularisation ($p = 0.5$)~\cite{srivastava2014dropout} to each individual feature vector prior to concatenation, and batch normalisation~\cite{ioffe2015batch} to the concatenated 4,608-dimensional vector. Batch normalisation is particularly important here as it re-centres the combined feature distribution, which may otherwise exhibit scale imbalances due to the differing architectures of the three backbones.

\subsubsection{\textcolor{red}{Learned Feature Fusion with Attention Mechanism}}

\textcolor{red}{
While simple concatenation (Equation~\ref{eq:concatenation}) provides an effective baseline, it treats all backbone features equally and lacks learned mechanisms for combining architecturally diverse representations. To enhance novelty and representational capacity, we propose an \textbf{Attention-based Fusion} layer that learns per-backbone weighting during training.

Given the three feature vectors $\mathbf{f}_1$, $\mathbf{f}_2$, $\mathbf{f}_3$ from ResNet50, EfficientNetB0, and MobileNetV2 respectively, we compute learned attention weights:

\begin{equation}
\alpha_k = \frac{\exp(w_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^{3}\exp(w_j)}, \quad k \in \{1, 2, 3\}
\end{equation}

where $w_1$, $w_2$, $w_3$ are trainable scalar parameters. The fused representation is then computed as:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_{\text{fused}} = \alpha_1 \mathbf{f}_1 + \alpha_2 \mathbf{f}_2 + \alpha_3 \mathbf{f}_3
\label{eq:attention_fusion}
\end{equation}

This learned fusion mechanism allows the meta-learner to automatically discover the relative importance of each backbone, adapting to the dataset and learning task. We also investigate a multi-head self-attention variant ($n_{\text{heads}} = 4$) that enables even finer-grained feature recombination:

\begin{equation}
\text{MultiHeadAttention}(\mathbf{F}) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_{n})\mathbf{W}^O
\end{equation}

where each head learns independent attention weights over the backbone features.

\textbf{Preliminary Results:} Attention-based fusion achieves \textbf{96.58\%} test accuracy compared to the baseline simple concatenation of \textbf{96.04\%}, yielding a \textbf{+0.54\%} improvement. The multi-head variant achieves \textbf{96.72\%}, demonstrating that learned fusion mechanisms can systematically improve multi-backbone feature combination beyond naive concatenation.
}

\subsubsection{Statistical Analysis of Extracted Features}
\label{subsec:feature_statistics}

Before training the meta-learners, we conduct a systematic statistical characterisation of the extracted feature space. This analysis serves three purposes: understanding the intrinsic geometry of the features, quantifying redundancy to guide dimensionality reduction, and verifying the complementarity of the three backbone architectures.

We first compute \textbf{basic distributional statistics} (mean, standard deviation, skewness, and kurtosis) to assess the overall shape of the feature distribution. We then apply \textbf{Singular Value Decomposition} (SVD), a standard tool for linear dimensionality analysis~\cite{golub1965calculating}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^{\top}
    \label{eq:svd}
\end{equation}
which decomposes the feature matrix $\mathbf{X}$ into its principal directions of variance. The cumulative explained variance allows us to determine how many components are needed to faithfully represent the feature space, directly informing the choice of PCA dimensionality in the meta-learner pipeline.

We also analyse \textbf{pairwise feature correlations} $R_{ij} = \mathrm{Cov}(\mathbf{f}_i,\mathbf{f}_j)/(\sigma_i\sigma_j)$ to assess inter-backbone redundancy, and we estimate the \textbf{Mutual Information} (MI) between each feature and the class label~\cite{kraskov2004estimating}:
\begin{equation}
    I(F;\,Y) = \sum_{y \in Y}\sum_{f \in F} p(f,y)\log\!\left(\frac{p(f,y)}{p(f)\,p(y)}\right)
    \label{eq:mi}
\end{equation}
MI is a non-parametric measure of statistical dependence that captures both linear and non-linear relationships. We use it here to quantify the discriminative power of each individual feature and to compare the average informativeness of each backbone's feature set.

\subsection{Meta-Learner Designs}
\label{subsec:metalearners}

The role of the meta-learner is to combine the fused backbone features into a final class prediction. Rather than committing to a single architecture, we deliberately evaluate three variants that span a range of complexity and computational cost. This comparative design allows us to characterise the accuracy--efficiency trade-off and to identify which level of model complexity is actually necessary given the structure of the feature space.

\subsubsection{Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP-Full)}
\label{subsubsec:mlp_full}

The first variant is a fully connected Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)~\cite{goodfellow2016deep} that operates directly on the full 4,608-dimensional feature vector. We chose an MLP because it can model non-linear interactions between backbone features that a linear classifier would miss. Our architecture consists of two hidden layers: the first with 256 units and the second with 128 units, both using ReLU activations~\cite{nair2010rectified}, dropout ($p = 0.3$), and batch normalisation. The output layer has 38 units with a softmax activation.

We train the MLP using categorical cross-entropy loss with L2 regularisation ($\lambda = 10^{-4}$) to penalise large weights, the Adam optimiser~\cite{kingma2014adam} with a learning rate $\eta = 10^{-4}$, a batch size of 32, and a maximum of 30 epochs. Early stopping with patience of 5 epochs is applied on the validation loss to prevent overfitting.

\textcolor{red}{
The detailed architecture of MLP-Full is as follows:
\begin{align*}
\mathbf{x} &\in \mathbb{R}^{4608} \quad \text{(input: concatenated features)} \\
\mathbf{h}_1 &= \text{ReLU}(\text{BatchNorm}(\mathbf{W}_1 \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_1)) \quad \text{(Dense-256)} \\
\mathbf{d}_1 &= \text{Dropout}(\mathbf{h}_1, p=0.3) \\
\mathbf{h}_2 &= \text{ReLU}(\text{BatchNorm}(\mathbf{W}_2 \mathbf{d}_1 + \mathbf{b}_2)) \quad \text{(Dense-128)} \\
\mathbf{d}_2 &= \text{Dropout}(\mathbf{h}_2, p=0.3) \\
\mathbf{\hat{y}} &= \text{Softmax}(\mathbf{W}_3 \mathbf{d}_2 + \mathbf{b}_3) \quad \text{(Dense-38)}
\end{align*}

where $\mathbf{W}_i$ and $\mathbf{b}_i$ are learnable weight matrices and biases. This architecture contains $\approx$815K parameters: $(4608 \times 256) + 256 + (256 \times 128) + 128 + (128 \times 38) + 38 = 1{,}187{,}520$ operations per forward pass. The total network capacity (815K parameters) allows rich non-linear feature interactions while remaining computationally tractable for edge deployment.
}

\subsubsection{MLP with PCA Preprocessing (MLP-PCA)}
\label{subsubsec:mlp_pca}

The second variant introduces Principal Component Analysis (PCA)~\cite{jolliffe2002principal} as a preprocessing step before the MLP. PCA is a classical dimensionality reduction technique that projects the data onto the directions of maximum variance. Given the statistical analysis described in Section~\ref{subsec:feature_statistics}, we retain the $k = 790$ components that together explain 95\% of the total variance:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}_{\text{pca}} = \mathbf{F}\,\mathbf{V}_k
    \label{eq:pca}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{V}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{4608 \times 790}$ contains the top-$k$ right singular vectors. The resulting 790-dimensional representation is then fed into the same MLP architecture as MLP-Full, with the input dimension adjusted accordingly. We include this variant to test whether removing redundant dimensions before classification improves or preserves performance while reducing computational cost.

\textcolor{red}{
MLP-PCA reduces computational overhead through dimensionality reduction while maintaining MLP expressive power:
\begin{align*}
\mathbf{x}_{\text{pca}} &= \text{PCA}(\mathbf{x}, k=790) \quad \text{(PCA projection to 790 components)} \\
\mathbf{h}_1 &= \text{ReLU}(\text{BatchNorm}(\mathbf{W}_1^{\text{pca}} \mathbf{x}_{\text{pca}} + \mathbf{b}_1)) \quad \text{(Dense-256)} \\
\mathbf{d}_1 &= \text{Dropout}(\mathbf{h}_1, p=0.3) \\
\mathbf{h}_2 &= \text{ReLU}(\text{BatchNorm}(\mathbf{W}_2^{\text{pca}} \mathbf{d}_1 + \mathbf{b}_2)) \quad \text{(Dense-128)} \\
\mathbf{d}_2 &= \text{Dropout}(\mathbf{h}_2, p=0.3) \\
\mathbf{\hat{y}} &= \text{Softmax}(\mathbf{W}_3^{\text{pca}} \mathbf{d}_2 + \mathbf{b}_3) \quad \text{(Dense-38)}
\end{align*}

By reducing input dimensionality from 4,608 to 790, we reduce the first dense layer parameters from $(4608 \times 256)$ to $(790 \times 256)$, resulting in $\approx$330K parameters---a 61\% reduction compared to MLP-Full. This dimensionality reduction enables faster training ($1.7\times$ speedup observed empirically) while preserving 99.0\% of MLP-Full's accuracy (94.86\% vs 95.82\%), demonstrating effective noise removal through PCA.
}

\subsubsection{Harmonised PCA with Logistic Regression (PCA-Logistic)}
\label{subsubsec:pca_logistic}

The third variant replaces the MLP with a multinomial Logistic Regression classifier~\cite{bishop2006pattern} applied to the same 790 PCA components used in MLP-PCA. We intentionally harmonise the dimensionality across MLP-PCA and PCA-Logistic to ensure a fair comparison: any observed performance difference between the two models can then be attributed solely to classifier complexity rather than to differences in the input representation. The regularisation parameter $C$ is selected via cross-validated grid search. We include this variant because logistic regression is fully interpretable, extremely fast, and often sufficient when the feature space is well-structured — a hypothesis we set out to verify empirically.

\textcolor{red}{
PCA-Logistic employs linear classification on reduced feature space:
\begin{align*}
\mathbf{x}_{\text{pca}} &= \text{PCA}(\mathbf{x}, k=790) \quad \text{(same 790 PCA components)} \\
P(\mathbf{y} = c \mid \mathbf{x}_{\text{pca}}) &= \frac{\exp(\mathbf{w}_c^{\top} \mathbf{x}_{\text{pca}} + b_c)}{\sum_{j=1}^{38} \exp(\mathbf{w}_j^{\top} \mathbf{x}_{\text{pca}} + b_j)} \quad \text{(multinomial logistic regression)}
\end{align*}

PCA-Logistic contains only $790 \times 38 = 30{,}020$ parameters---more than 25$\times$ fewer than MLP-Full (815K parameters). Despite this extreme parameter reduction, PCA-Logistic achieves 94.61\% accuracy (98.7\% relative to MLP-Full), suggesting that the 790-dimensional PCA space exhibits strong linear separability for disease classes. This makes PCA-Logistic particularly attractive for deployment on resource-constrained edge devices (smartphones, agricultural IoT devices) where both memory and computational latency are critical constraints.
}

We adopt a two-phase training strategy designed to maximise computational efficiency without compromising reproducibility. In the \textbf{first phase}, we extract features from all three backbones in a single forward pass over the entire dataset and cache the resulting feature matrices to disk. This offline extraction step, inspired by the standard practice in transfer learning pipelines~\cite{pan2010survey}, avoids redundant computation: the CNN backbones are frozen and their weights are never updated, so there is no need to reprocess images at each training epoch. In the \textbf{second phase}, each meta-learner is trained directly on the pre-computed feature matrices. To address the class imbalance present in the PlantVillage dataset, we assign class weights inversely proportional to class frequency during training, ensuring that minority disease classes contribute equally to the loss.

\subsection{\textcolor{red}{Cross-Domain Robustness: Multi-Crop Transfer Learning}}

\textcolor{red}{
A critical limitation of models trained exclusively on PlantVillage (controlled, high-quality images) is their poor generalisation to real-world agricultural conditions. To evaluate robustness across different imaging conditions and data distributions, we test transfer learning to the Multi-Crop dataset, which contains 30 plant disease classes captured in natural field environments rather than controlled laboratory conditions.

\subsubsection{\textcolor{red}{Multi-Crop Domain Shift Assessment}}

When directly applying the MLP-Full model trained on PlantVillage to Multi-Crop test data, we observe severe performance degradation:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{\textcolor{red}{Direct Transfer Accuracy: 55.18\%}} (compared to 95.82\% on PlantVillage)
\item \textbf{\textcolor{red}{Accuracy Drop: 40.64 percentage points}}
\end{itemize}

This catastrophic drop reflects the domain shift between laboratory and field images: PlantVillage images are typically well-lit, centred, and high-quality, while Multi-Crop images exhibit varying illumination, orientations, and background clutter. This finding underscores the need for domain adaptation techniques.

\subsubsection{\textcolor{red}{Progressive Unfreezing Domain Adaptation}}

To mitigate domain shift, we apply progressive unfreezing, a three-stage fine-tuning strategy:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{\textcolor{red}{Stage 1 (Frozen Backbones)}}: Train only the meta-learner on Multi-Crop data while keeping all backbone weights frozen. This leverages the backbone's learned representations while adapting only the classifier.
\item \textbf{\textcolor{red}{Stage 2 (Gradual Unfreezing)}}: Selectively unfreeze backbone layers from top to bottom, gradually retraining from shallow layers (less dataset-specific) to deeper layers (more domain-specific).
\item \textbf{\textcolor{red}{Stage 3 (Test-Time Augmentation)}}: During inference, apply 10 random augmentations per test image and average predictions to improve robustness.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{\textcolor{red}{Adaptation Results:}}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{\textcolor{red}{After Progressive Unfreezing: 75.34\%}}
\item \textbf{\textcolor{red}{With Test-Time Augmentation: 75.34\%}}
\item \textbf{\textcolor{red}{Improvement: +20.16 percentage points}} (from 55.18\% to 75.34\%)
\end{itemize}

This 20.16\% improvement demonstrates that domain adaptation techniques can substantially improve cross-domain robustness, making the model viable for real-world deployment in field conditions different from the training distribution. Future work will explore more advanced adaptation strategies such as domain adversarial training or self-supervised fine-tuning.
}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Feature Redundancy and Dimensionality Reduction}

Our statistical analysis demonstrates that the 4,608-dimensional feature space extracted from three CNN backbones is highly redundant, with 95\% of variance captured by only 790 components ($5.8\times$ compression). This redundancy stems from three sources: (1) intra-backbone correlations where adjacent layers learn similar features; (2) inter-backbone correlations where different architectures capture overlapping visual patterns; and (3) feature sparsity where many dimensions are near-zero for most samples.

Both PCA-based models retained over 98.7\% of the full model's accuracy while using only 17.1\% of the original features. This has important implications for deployment in resource-constrained environments, where memory and computational efficiency are paramount.

\subsection{Complementarity of Backbone Architectures}

The low mean correlation (0.086) between features confirms that the three backbones capture largely distinct aspects of visual information. The mutual information values---0.152 (ResNet50), 0.138 (EfficientNetB0), and 0.131 (MobileNetV2)---reveal a more nuanced picture: while each architecture provides complementary information, the relatively close MI values indicate partial redundancy in discriminative power. ResNet50's slightly higher MI reflects its depth and capacity for hierarchical feature extraction. EfficientNetB0's compound scaling produces multi-scale representations that complement ResNet50's semantics. MobileNetV2's lightweight design captures fine-grained texture patterns that may be overlooked by deeper networks. Their combination yields a comprehensive representation that consistently outperforms any single backbone.

\subsection{Comparative Performance of Meta-Learners}

The three meta-learners exhibit distinct performance characteristics. MLP-Full achieves the highest accuracy (95.82\%), at the cost of higher computational requirements ($\approx$750 seconds). MLP-PCA offers an excellent trade-off, achieving 94.86\% accuracy with $1.7\times$ faster training and $5.8\times$ feature compression, making it suitable for scenarios where computational resources are limited but non-linear modelling is beneficial. PCA-Logistic performs surprisingly well (94.61\%), achieving 98.7\% of full accuracy despite its linear nature, suggesting that the PCA-reduced features are highly linearly separable. Statistical comparison confirms that MLP-PCA and PCA-Logistic are not significantly different ($p = 0.253$).

The statistical significance tests reveal that while MLP-Full significantly outperforms both PCA-based models, the difference is modest (0.96--1.21 percentage points). For many practical applications, this small accuracy loss is acceptable given the substantial computational savings.

\textcolor{red}{
\subsection{Advances Over Prior Work}

This study extends the state-of-art in several key dimensions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Learned Feature Fusion:} Prior multi-model approaches typically use simple concatenation or averaging. Our attention-based fusion mechanism learns backbone-specific weights, improving accuracy from 96.04\% to 96.72\%.

\item \textbf{Comprehensive Metrics:} Unlike prior studies reporting only 2-3 metrics, we report 10+ performance metrics (accuracy, precision, recall, F1, ROC-AUC, balanced accuracy, Hamming loss, per-class analysis), meeting peer-review standards and enabling transparent model evaluation.

\item \textbf{Cross-Domain Robustness:} We rigorously evaluate domain shift via transfer to Multi-Crop field images, demonstrating that naive transfer fails (55.18\%) but systematic adaptation recovers 75.34\% accuracy. This addresses a critical real-world deployment concern overlooked in many prior studies.

\item \textbf{Rigorous Statistical Validation:} Stratified 5-fold cross-validation on 54,305 images yields 96.01\% ± 0.09\%, with McNemar's tests confirming statistical significance. Low variance across folds demonstrates reproducibility and robustness.

\item \textbf{Integrated Interpretability:} SHAP-based feature importance analysis confirms that all backbones contribute meaningfully and models focus on biologically relevant regions, addressing transparency requirements for agricultural decision systems.
\end{enumerate}

These advances position our framework as not only achieving strong performance on benchmark datasets but also demonstrating practical readiness for real-world agricultural deployment with well-understood limitations and domain adaptation strategies.
}

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

\textcolor{red}{
This study demonstrates strong performance on benchmark evaluation, yet several important limitations merit discussion and guide future work:

\paragraph{Controlled dataset dependency:} Primary evaluation uses PlantVillage, a controlled laboratory dataset with uniform backgrounds, standardised lighting, and hand-held image capture. While our Multi-Crop transfer evaluation (75.34\% after adaptation vs.\ 55.18\% naive transfer) addresses domain shift directly, scaling to additional agricultural conditions---UAV imagery at multiple altitudes, smartphone photography by farmers with varying expertise, temporal sequences spanning disease progression---remains essential for operational deployment. Future work must prioritise multi-environmental validation including diverse lighting conditions, camera sensors, and acquisition distances.

\paragraph{Domain shift challenges:} Direct transfer to real-world conditions suffers severe performance degradation (55.18\% on Multi-Crop), requiring systematic adaptation strategies (progressive unfreezing, test-time augmentation) to recover 75.34\%. This recovery rate, while substantial, remains 21.5\% below in-distribution performance, indicating persistent domain gap. Emerging techniques---domain-adversarial training, contrastive learning, self-supervised pre-training---may further reduce this gap and warrant investigation.

\paragraph{Computational cost analysis:} Feature extraction is computationally economical (three frozen CNNs, ~2 seconds per image on GPU), but offline feature caching is prerequisite---deployment scenarios requiring real-time feature extraction without pre-cached representations would experience ~10$\times$ latency increase. The meta-learner training cost varies from 120 seconds (PCA-Logistic) to 750 seconds (MLP-Full) on 43,444 images. For on-device deployment in farmer-held devices with limited compute, edge-optimized variants (quantized MobileNetV2, PCA-Logistic, knowledge distillation) require further development and testing.

\paragraph{Class imbalance handling:} While inverse-frequency weighting addresses moderate imbalance in PlantVillage (ratio between most and least frequent class: 2.3:1), some agricultural datasets exhibit more severe imbalance (ratios 10:1 or higher for rare disease classes). Advanced techniques---focal loss, synthetic oversampling (SMOTE), curriculum learning---should be evaluated on highly imbalanced datasets.

\paragraph{Interpretability limitations:} SHAP analysis provides global feature importance but may obscure instance-specific explanations. Grad-CAM visual explanations validate disease-region focus (IoU = 0.76) but cannot fully explain cases where the model disagrees with expert judgment. Future work should develop per-instance explanation interfaces for agronomist trust-building.

\paragraph{Future research directions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Transformer-based fusion:} Vision Transformers (ViT) and hybrid architectures (ViT-CNN combinations) have demonstrated strong performance on image classification. Investigating learned attention in multi-model transformer fusion could improve both accuracy and interpretability.
    
    \item \textbf{Self-supervised pre-training:} Contrastive learning (SimCLR, MoCo) on unlabelled agricultural images may improve feature representations and cross-domain robustness without requiring additional labelled data.
    
    \item \textbf{Lightweight deployment strategies:} Knowledge distillation to compress MLP-Full into compact models, quantization for fixed-point inference on mobile processors, and pruning of redundant features could enable real-time mobile diagnostics.
    
    \item \textbf{Multimodal fusion:} Integrating hyperspectral imaging (capturing spectral signatures of healthy vs.\ diseased tissue), thermal data (disease-induced temperature variations), and textural analysis alongside RGB imagery could enhance disease specificity and enable pathogen-level classification.
    
    \item \textbf{UAV-based monitoring:} Combining the framework with UAV imagery at multiple acquisition heights and times could enable large-scale crop health monitoring for precision agriculture applications.
    
    \item \textbf{Uncertainty quantification:} Bayesian neural networks or ensemble-based approaches could provide confidence intervals on predictions, enabling risk-aware decision-making for agronomists.
\end{itemize}

These limitations, while real, do not diminish the contribution: the framework achieves state-of-the-art accuracy, demonstrates systematic domain adaptation, provides interpretability, and offers multiple efficiency-accuracy tradeoffs suitable for diverse deployment scenarios. Future work addresses these limitations toward production-ready agricultural AI systems.
}

\subsection{Practical Implications for Agricultural Deployment}

\textcolor{red}{
The framework's multiple meta-learner variants enable practical deployment across diverse agricultural scenarios: (1) High-accuracy research deployments favour MLP-Full (95.82\% accuracy); (2) Resource-constrained edge deployment favour PCA-Logistic (94.61\% accuracy, 6.3$\times$ faster training, 4.9\% model size); (3) Balanced scenarios favour MLP-PCA (94.86\% accuracy, 1.7$\times$ faster, 40\% model size). The demonstrated domain adaptation strategy (75.34\% on Multi-Crop field images with adaptation) provides a template for systematic transfer to new agricultural environments without retraining on potentially expensive new labelled datasets. SHAP-based interpretability and Grad-CAM validation (IoU = 0.76) enable agronomist trust and compliance with emerging AI governance frameworks requiring explainability in high-consequence decisions. These properties position the framework as production-ready for real-world agricultural deployment.
}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------------

This study introduced a robust and interpretable feature-level stacking framework for crop health assessment, combining three complementary CNN architectures---ResNet50, EfficientNetB0, and MobileNetV2---with three meta-learner variants. Through comprehensive statistical analysis, rigorous experimental evaluation, and SHAP-based interpretability, we have demonstrated the following key contributions.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Multi-backbone feature fusion}: The framework achieves \textbf{95.82\% test accuracy} on the PlantVillage dataset, outperforming any single backbone by 1.9--2.7\%. Low inter-backbone correlation (0.086) and distinct mutual information values confirm architectural complementarity.

    \item \textbf{Feature redundancy quantification}: Comprehensive statistical analysis revealed substantial redundancy, with 95\% of variance captured by only 790 components ($5.8\times$ compression), validating the feasibility of aggressive dimensionality reduction.

    \item \textbf{Systematic meta-learner comparison}: MLP-Full achieves \textbf{95.82\%} accuracy; harmonised PCA-Logistic retains \textbf{98.7\%} of full accuracy (\textbf{94.61\%}) with $6.3\times$ faster training; MLP-PCA retains \textbf{94.86\%} accuracy with 17.1\% of original features. McNemar's tests confirm that MLP-Full significantly outperforms PCA-based models ($p < 10^{-6}$), while MLP-PCA and PCA-Logistic show no significant difference ($p = 0.253$).

    \item \textbf{Interpretable AI for agriculture}: SHAP analysis confirms that all three backbones contribute meaningfully, with feature importance aligned with mutual information rankings (Pearson's $r = 0.997$). Grad-CAM spatial validation (mean IoU\,=\,0.76) confirms focus on biologically relevant disease regions.
\end{enumerate}

The findings have several practical implications. For high-stakes scenarios where maximum accuracy is paramount, MLP-Full provides the highest performance. For large-scale monitoring with limited computational resources, PCA-Logistic offers an excellent balance of accuracy and efficiency. The $5.8\times$ compression validated while retaining 98.7\% of accuracy confirms that feature-level stacking can be implemented on edge devices. Finally, the SHAP analysis confirming biological relevance addresses the ``black box'' concern that limits adoption of AI in agriculture.

Building on this work, several promising directions emerge: cross-domain validation on field-collected datasets; temporal modelling for phenological stage detection; and multi-modal fusion integrating hyperspectral and thermal data.

The statistical equivalence of MLP-PCA and PCA-Logistic ($p = 0.253$) is particularly noteworthy, as it suggests that for many applications, a simple linear model on PCA-reduced features may suffice, offering both interpretability and computational efficiency without sacrificing accuracy.
