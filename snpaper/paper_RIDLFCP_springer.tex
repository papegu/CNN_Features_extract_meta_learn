\abstract{
\textcolor{red}{Accurate crop health assessment is critical for food security in climate-vulnerable agricultural regions. While deep learning has demonstrated strong potential for plant disease detection, most existing approaches rely on single-model architectures and limited evaluation protocols, restricting both generalisation and interpretability.} We propose a hybrid feature-level stacking framework that combines representations extracted from ResNet50, EfficientNetB0, and MobileNetV2, followed by meta-learning using MLP-Full, MLP-PCA, and PCA-Logistic classifiers. \textcolor{red}{Evaluated on the PlantVillage dataset (54,306 images, 38 disease classes), the framework achieves \textbf{96.04\%} test accuracy with MLP-Full, while MLP-PCA and PCA-Logistic reach \textbf{95.22\%} and \textbf{95.27\%}, respectively, using only 17.1\% of the original feature dimensions. Statistical analysis of the extracted feature space reveals substantial redundancy (5.83$\times$ compression at 95\% variance) alongside low inter-backbone correlation (mean $|r| = 0.091$), confirming the complementarity of the three architectures.} These results confirm that hybrid feature-level stacking yields strong predictive performance while preserving model flexibility across meta-learner choices.
}

\keywords{Feature-level stacking, Convolutional Neural Networks, Plant disease detection, Model interpretability, Agricultural image analysis}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------------

Accurate assessment of crop health and yield potential remains a major challenge in modern agriculture, particularly in regions with high climatic variability such as Senegal. The country experiences recurrent droughts, irregular rainfall patterns, and increasing pressures on agricultural productivity, making early diagnosis of crop diseases and stress conditions essential for food security and sustainable development.

In recent years, deep learning---particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)---has emerged as a powerful tool for plant disease detection and crop monitoring using image data. For instance, several studies have demonstrated that transfer learning and fine-tuning of pre-trained architectures can significantly outperform traditional handcrafted feature methods.

Building on this momentum, several comparative studies have benchmarked popular CNN architectures for crop health monitoring. Beyond ground-based datasets, UAV-based remote sensing has also been explored, highlighting the importance of robust and transferable visual representations.

Despite these advances, most existing methods rely on \textbf{single-model architectures}---such as ResNet50, EfficientNet, or MobileNetV2---which often fail to generalise effectively across different image distributions, disease types, and field conditions. A single backbone may capture only a subset of the relevant semantic and textural information needed for robust crop health assessment.

To address these challenges, this study proposes a \textbf{hybrid feature-level stacking framework} that combines multiple pre-trained CNN models to improve generalisation and predictive performance.

\textcolor{red}{Our contribution addresses two complementary dimensions: a methodological one, in which multi-backbone feature stacking combines semantically rich, multi-scale, and lightweight representations into a unified 4,608-dimensional embedding; and an empirical one, in which systematic meta-learner comparison provides reproducible quantitative evidence across three distinct classifiers.}

The main contributions of this work are fourfold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{multi-backbone feature fusion} approach that leverages complementary representations from ResNet50, EfficientNetB0, and MobileNetV2.

    \item \textbf{Comprehensive statistical analysis} revealing substantial feature redundancy (\textcolor{red}{$5.83\times$} compression at 95\% variance) and low inter-backbone correlation (\textcolor{red}{0.091}), confirming architecture complementarity.

    \item \textbf{Systematic evaluation of meta-learners} demonstrating important trade-offs: \textcolor{red}{MLP-Full (\textbf{96.04\% accuracy}), MLP-PCA (\textbf{95.22\%}), and PCA-Logistic (\textbf{95.27\%})}.

    \item \textbf{Integrated explainability} confirming that all three backbones contribute meaningfully to classification decisions, with models focusing on biologically relevant regions.
\end{enumerate}

The proposed framework addresses key limitations of existing approaches by enhancing robustness through multi-model feature fusion, providing transparency through integrated explainability, and enabling systematic comparison between full and reduced feature-space meta-learners.

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} details our methodology, including data preprocessing, multi-backbone feature extraction, statistical feature analysis, and the design of the three meta-learners. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the experimental findings. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the broader implications, limitations, and future directions, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

In the first stage, three distinct CNN architectures---ResNet50, EfficientNetB0, and MobileNetV2---extract deep features from the same input image. These features are then concatenated and passed to one of three meta-learners for final classification.

\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}
\label{subsec:preprocessing}

We use the PlantVillage dataset~\cite{plantvillage}, a benchmark collection for agricultural disease classification containing 54,306 images across 38 classes of healthy and diseased plant leaves.

\subsubsection{Data Augmentation}

A well-known challenge in supervised learning is the risk of overfitting, particularly when training data is limited or imbalanced. Data augmentation has been widely adopted as an effective remedy, increasing the diversity of the training set without requiring additional manual annotation.

For \textbf{geometric transformations}, we apply random horizontal and vertical flips (probability 0.5), random rotations up to $25^\circ$, random translations up to 10\% of the image dimensions, and random zoom.

For \textbf{photometric transformations}, we apply random contrast adjustment, random brightness modification, random saturation adjustment, and additive Gaussian noise.

For \textbf{colour augmentation}, we apply a random hue adjustment to account for spectral shifts caused by different times of day and weather conditions.

After augmentation, all images are resized to $224 \times 224$ pixels and normalised to the range $[0, 1]$. We then apply per-image standardisation to centre and scale each sample independently:
\begin{equation}
    I_{\text{norm}} = \frac{I - \mu_I}{\sigma_I + \epsilon}
    \label{eq:normalization}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_I$ and $\sigma_I$ denote the per-image mean and standard deviation respectively, and $\epsilon = 10^{-7}$ is a small constant that prevents numerical instability.

\subsubsection{Dataset Splitting}

To obtain unbiased performance estimates, we partition the dataset into three non-overlapping subsets using stratified splitting, which preserves the class distribution across all splits:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Training set}: 80\% (43,444 images) — used to train all meta-learner variants.
    \item \textbf{Validation set}: 10\% (5,431 images) — used for hyperparameter selection and early stopping.
    \item \textbf{Test set}: 10\% (5,431 images) — held out entirely and used only for final evaluation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Feature Extraction with Pre-trained CNNs}
\label{subsec:feature_extraction}

Transfer learning has become the dominant paradigm for image classification tasks where labelled data is scarce. For each backbone, we remove the final classification layer and attach a Global Average Pooling (GAP) operation:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{f}_k = \mathrm{GAP}\!\left(\mathrm{Backbone}_k(\mathbf{x})\right)
    \label{eq:feature_extraction}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}$ is the input image and $\mathbf{f}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{d_k}$, with $d_{\text{ResNet}} = 2048$, $d_{\text{Efficient}} = 1280$, and $d_{\text{Mobile}} = 1280$.

\subsubsection{Feature Concatenation and Regularisation}

Once extracted, the three feature vectors are concatenated into a single unified representation:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F} = [\mathbf{f}_1;\, \mathbf{f}_2;\, \mathbf{f}_3] \in \mathbb{R}^{4608}
    \label{eq:concatenation}
\end{equation}

This feature-level fusion strategy, also known as early fusion, allows the meta-learner to jointly exploit all available backbone representations.

\subsubsection{\textcolor{red}{Learned Feature Fusion with Attention Mechanism}}

\textcolor{red}{While simple concatenation (Equation~\ref{eq:concatenation}) provides an effective baseline, it treats all backbone features equally and lacks a learned mechanism for balancing their relative contributions. An attention-based extension can be defined by assigning trainable scalar weights to each backbone representation:}

\textcolor{red}{
\begin{equation}
\alpha_k = \frac{\exp(w_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^{3}\exp(w_j)}, \quad k \in \{1, 2, 3\}
\end{equation}
}

\textcolor{red}{The fused representation is then}

\textcolor{red}{
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_{\text{fused}} = \alpha_1 \mathbf{f}_1 + \alpha_2 \mathbf{f}_2 + \alpha_3 \mathbf{f}_3
\label{eq:attention_fusion}
\end{equation}
}

\textcolor{red}{This formulation is presented as a natural extension of the baseline stacking pipeline and motivates future evaluation of adaptive fusion mechanisms.}

\subsubsection{Statistical Analysis of Extracted Features}
\label{subsec:feature_statistics}

Before training the meta-learners, we conduct a systematic statistical characterisation of the extracted feature space. This analysis serves three purposes: understanding the intrinsic geometry of the representation, quantifying redundancy, and assessing discriminative power.

We first compute \textbf{basic distributional statistics} (mean, standard deviation, skewness, and kurtosis) to assess the overall shape of the feature distribution. We then apply \textbf{Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^{\top}
    \label{eq:svd}
\end{equation}
which decomposes the feature matrix $\mathbf{X}$ into its principal directions of variance.

We also analyse \textbf{pairwise feature correlations} and estimate the \textbf{Mutual Information (MI)} between each feature and the class label:
\begin{equation}
    I(F;\,Y) = \sum_{y \in Y}\sum_{f \in F} p(f,y)\log\!\left(\frac{p(f,y)}{p(f)\,p(y)}\right)
    \label{eq:mi}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Meta-Learner Designs}
\label{subsec:metalearners}

The role of the meta-learner is to combine the fused backbone features into a final class prediction. We evaluate three variants.

\subsubsection{Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP-Full)}
\label{subsubsec:mlp_full}

The first variant is a fully connected Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) that operates directly on the full 4,608-dimensional feature vector.

\textcolor{red}{The detailed architecture of MLP-Full is retained here to improve architectural transparency and reproducibility:}

\textcolor{red}{
\begin{align*}
\mathbf{x} &\in \mathbb{R}^{4608} \\
\mathbf{h}_1 &= \text{ReLU}(\text{BatchNorm}(\mathbf{W}_1 \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_1)) \\
\mathbf{d}_1 &= \text{Dropout}(\mathbf{h}_1, p=0.3) \\
\mathbf{h}_2 &= \text{ReLU}(\text{BatchNorm}(\mathbf{W}_2 \mathbf{d}_1 + \mathbf{b}_2)) \\
\mathbf{d}_2 &= \text{Dropout}(\mathbf{h}_2, p=0.3) \\
\hat{\mathbf{y}} &= \text{Softmax}(\mathbf{W}_3 \mathbf{d}_2 + \mathbf{b}_3)
\end{align*}
}

\subsubsection{MLP with PCA Preprocessing (MLP-PCA)}
\label{subsubsec:mlp_pca}

The second variant introduces Principal Component Analysis (PCA) as a preprocessing step before the MLP:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}_{\text{pca}} = \mathbf{F}\,\mathbf{V}_k
    \label{eq:pca}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{V}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{4608 \times 790}$ contains the top-$k$ right singular vectors.

\textcolor{red}{The dimensionality reduction setting is explicitly aligned with the feature statistics, using 790 components to preserve 95\% of the variance.}

\subsubsection{Harmonised PCA with Logistic Regression (PCA-Logistic)}
\label{subsubsec:pca_logistic}

The third variant replaces the MLP with a multinomial Logistic Regression classifier applied to the same 790 PCA components used in MLP-PCA.

\textcolor{red}{This harmonised formulation ensures that the comparison between MLP-PCA and PCA-Logistic reflects the influence of the classifier family itself rather than inconsistent dimensionality choices.}

\subsection{Summary of Key Findings}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Feature redundancy and complementarity}: The 4,608-dimensional feature space exhibits substantial redundancy (\textcolor{red}{5.83$\times$ compression at 95\% variance}), yet inter-backbone correlations remain low enough to justify feature fusion.
    \item \textbf{Superior performance of feature fusion}: MLP-Full achieves \textcolor{red}{\textbf{96.04\%}} accuracy on the \textcolor{red}{held-out PlantVillage test set}.
    \item \textbf{Accuracy--efficiency trade-offs}: PCA-Logistic and MLP-PCA remain highly competitive while relying on the reduced PCA space.
    \item \textbf{Statistical validation}: McNemar's tests confirm that MLP-Full is significantly better than PCA-based models, while MLP-PCA and PCA-Logistic are statistically equivalent.
    \item \textbf{Interpretable decisions}: SHAP feature importance aligns strongly with mutual information rankings.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Feature Redundancy and Dimensionality Reduction}

Our statistical analysis demonstrates that the 4,608-dimensional feature space extracted from three CNN backbones is highly redundant, with 95\% of variance captured by only 790 components (\textcolor{red}{$5.83\times$ compression}).

Both PCA-based models retained most of the full model's accuracy while using only 17.1\% of the original features. This has important implications for deployment in resource-constrained environments.

\subsection{Complementarity of Backbone Architectures}

The \textcolor{red}{low mean correlation (0.091)} between features confirms that the three backbones capture largely distinct aspects of visual information. The mutual information values further support their complementarity.

\subsection{Comparative Performance of Meta-Learners}

The three meta-learners exhibit a consistent accuracy--efficiency gradient. \textcolor{red}{MLP-Full achieves the strongest held-out test performance (96.04\%),} but requires training over all 4,608 features, implying higher memory and computational costs. MLP-PCA and PCA-Logistic trade a modest accuracy reduction for a substantial reduction in feature dimensionality and shorter training times.

The statistical significance tests reveal that while MLP-Full significantly outperforms both PCA-based models, the difference is modest. For many practical applications, the reduced-dimension alternatives may therefore offer an advantageous compromise between speed and accuracy.

\subsection{Advances Over Prior Work}

\textcolor{red}{The framework extends prior plant-disease classification pipelines by combining complementary CNN backbones at the feature level, by explicitly quantifying redundancy and complementarity in the fused representation, and by demonstrating that both nonlinear and linear meta-learners can exploit that representation effectively.}

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

\textcolor{red}{This study demonstrates strong performance on benchmark evaluation, yet several important limitations remain. The strongest evidence currently concerns the in-domain PlantVillage setting. Future work should therefore further consolidate cross-domain validation, adaptive fusion strategies, and broader robustness analyses under field conditions and repeated-split evaluation protocols.}

\subsection{Practical Implications for Agricultural Deployment}

\textcolor{red}{The framework's multiple meta-learner variants enable practical deployment across diverse agricultural scenarios: high-accuracy deployments may favour MLP-Full, while resource-constrained settings may benefit from PCA-based alternatives that retain most of the predictive performance.}

%%--------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------------

This study introduced a robust and interpretable feature-level stacking framework for crop health assessment, combining three complementary CNN architectures---ResNet50, EfficientNetB0, and MobileNetV2---within a unified meta-learning pipeline.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Multi-backbone feature fusion}: The framework achieves \textcolor{red}{\textbf{96.04\% test accuracy}} on the PlantVillage test set, outperforming the PCA-based alternatives while preserving the benefits of complementary feature extraction.

    \item \textbf{Feature redundancy quantification}: Comprehensive statistical analysis revealed substantial redundancy, with 95\% of variance captured by only 790 components (\textcolor{red}{$5.83\times$ compression}).

    \item \textbf{Systematic meta-learner comparison}: \textcolor{red}{MLP-Full reaches 96.04\%, MLP-PCA 95.22\%, and PCA-Logistic 95.27\%,} highlighting a clear yet practically modest trade-off between performance and efficiency.

    \item \textbf{Interpretable AI for agriculture}: SHAP analysis confirms that all three backbones contribute meaningfully, with feature importance aligned with mutual information rankings.
\end{enumerate}

The findings have several practical implications. For high-stakes scenarios where maximum accuracy is paramount, MLP-Full provides the strongest held-out result. For large-scale monitoring with limited resources, the PCA-based alternatives remain attractive due to their reduced dimensionality and lower computational requirements.

\textcolor{red}{These results show that hybrid feature-level stacking can simultaneously provide strong predictive performance, interpretable backbone complementarity, and flexible deployment trade-offs for crop-health assessment.}
